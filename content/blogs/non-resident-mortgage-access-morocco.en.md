# Mortgage Access in Morocco for Non-Resident Foreigners: Conditions and Restrictions

Morocco has long been an attractive destination for foreign investors, retirees, and expatriates seeking to acquire real estate. Whether for a holiday home in Marrakech or a commercial venture in Casablanca, understanding the legal framework for financing such acquisitions is crucial. Moroccan law provides specific mechanisms for non-residents to access credit, though these are governed by strict exchange control regulations and banking standards.

Navigating the intersection of property law and financial regulations requires an understanding of how the Moroccan state guarantees certain loans and how "Offshore" banking entities operate within the Kingdom. This article explores the legal conditions, the role of state guarantees, and the protections offered to foreign investors under Moroccan law.

## The Legal Framework for Foreign Real Estate Financing

In Morocco, the primary legislation governing property finance includes the **Royal Decree (Decret Royal) No. 552.67**, which serves as the foundational law for the **Crédit Immobilier et Hôtelier (CIH)**. This institution was specifically established to facilitate mortgage lending for construction and hospitality projects.

For non-residents, mortgage access is often facilitated through two channels:
1. **Local Moroccan Banks:** Financing provided in Moroccan Dirhams (MAD).
2. **Offshore Banks:** Regulated by **Law No. 58.90**, these entities allow for financing in convertible foreign currencies.

Under **Law No. 58.90, Article 14**, offshore banks have the freedom to conduct all financial and banking operations in convertible foreign currencies for the account of non-resident natural or legal persons. However, if a non-resident wishes to obtain credit from a local Moroccan resident bank, they generally require prior authorization from the **Office des Changes** (Exchange Office).

## State Guarantees and Institutional Lending

The Moroccan government frequently intervenes to stabilize the mortgage market and encourage investment through state-backed guarantees. Several decrees have historically empowered institutions like the CIH to issue loans with the state acting as a guarantor.

For example, **Reference 3 and 4** cite decrees where the state guarantees loans issued by the CIH up to specific limits (e.g., 400 million or 1 billion Dirhams). While these specific decrees often target the institution's liquidity to allow it to lend to the public, they demonstrate the Moroccan state's commitment to ensuring the solvency of the mortgage sector.

For a foreign investor, this means that the Moroccan banking system is structurally supported by the state, providing a layer of security for long-term mortgage contracts. Furthermore, the **Finance Act of 2008 (Loi de Finances 2008)** highlights the state’s role in managing loans granted to various sectors, including banking institutions, ensuring that the flow of credit remains regulated and sustainable.

## Exchange Control and the "Convertibility Arrangement"

One of the most critical aspects for non-resident foreigners is the "Convertibility Arrangement" (Régime de Convertibilité). Under **Investment Charter Law No. 18.95**, specifically **Article 9**, foreign investors who finance their projects in foreign currency enjoy a guarantee of convertibility.

This legal protection ensures that:
*   **Repatriation of Funds:** If the property is sold, the investor has the right to transfer the proceeds of the sale (including capital gains) out of Morocco in foreign currency.
*   **Profit Transfer:** Net profits from the investment can be transferred abroad without limit on amount or duration.

To benefit from these protections, it is vital that the initial investment—including the down payment for a mortgage—is transferred from abroad through a "convertible Dirham" account. This creates a paper trail that the Office des Changes uses to authorize future outward transfers.

## Practical Requirements for Non-Resident Loans

When an expat or non-resident applies for a mortgage in Morocco, the following conditions typically apply:

1.  **Down Payment:** Non-residents are generally required to provide a higher down payment compared to residents, often ranging from 30% to 50% of the property value.
2.  **Currency of Loan:** Loans can be issued in Dirhams if the borrower has a local income, but most non-residents opt for loans linked to convertible accounts.
3.  **Collateral and Guarantees:** Per **Decree Law regarding "Moroccanization" loans (Reference 8)**, loans are generally not granted until specific guarantees are established in favour of the state or the intervening banking institution. In standard mortgages, the property itself serves as the primary collateral (hypothèque).
4.  **Insurance:** Borrowers must typically subscribe to life and disability insurance, as well as property insurance, often through a Moroccan provider.

## Conclusion and Key Takeaways

Securing a mortgage in Morocco as a non-resident is a regulated but well-established process. The legal framework, supported by the Investment Charter and specific banking laws, provides a secure environment for foreign capital.

**Key Takeaways:**
*   **Legal Basis:** Mortgage lending is governed by the Royal Decree No. 552.67 and Law No. 58.90 for offshore entities.
*   **Convertibility is Key:** Always ensure your investment enters Morocco through the proper banking channels to guarantee your right to repatriate funds later.
*   **State Oversight:** The Moroccan state frequently guarantees institutional lending to ensure market stability, as seen in various Finance Acts and specific decrees.
*   **Regulatory Approval:** Most credit operations for non-residents involve the oversight of the Office des Changes to manage foreign currency flows.
